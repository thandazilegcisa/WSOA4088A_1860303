Defining Gamification

According to (Deterding 2016) the term gamification began being conceptualised in the year 2008. Since then, the proliferation of “gamified” media has led researchers to describe “gamification” in relation to the adoption of game design elements outside the gaming industry. Although much contestation around the term has largely been rather controversial, as there is no previous agreement about what gamification is. Hence, the abundance of published research in game studies and proliferation of user experience designers as a profession has resulted in referring to gamification as using interactive technologies and game design principles to improve audience and employee participation and engagement.

Hence, to my discretion, how gamification has been referred to and thought of, is that it would embody the ‘aura’ and culture of a product. And that not only would users exhibit value in the product itself, but the phenomenological experience gained from the use. I refer to this because several researchers have examined “playfulness as a desirable user experience or mode of interaction and how to design for it”. In the field of game studies, “gamification” can be seen through the lens of serious and persuasive games that entangles the game experience with real-life – the personal body instead of a fictional body.

However, mediums outside games such as film, animation, news, reality tv, and music often describe gamification about the process of using game design modes – UI interactions, design principles, and elements. And integrating ‘gamified’ modes of representation into the broader extensions of the provisions of digital technology in the form of e-commerce, health, education services, and interactive art. Gamification and its integration in the practice and mastery of art particularly interested me. And upon reading the literature, it relied heavily on theory and theoretical research which thus did not produce an ample sum of examples to infer to, therefore, the question that kept on continuously ravaging my mind Is whether gamification is synonymous with intermediality?

Deterding et al (2016) begins’ by defining the term gamification through a series of sub-sections detailing what is meant by a game, a game element, and non-game context. That is important because by defining what a game means I was able to promptly differentiate between gamification and intermediality. Intermediality refers to something that mixes modes and mediums to create something new. It is a way of understanding the relations or transgressions between two media. It is for this reason that I fell into probably the same misconceptions that people outside the gaming industry may have.

Therefore, to elaborate on this distinction, I will use a recent (2021) Rick and Morty special episode. Rick and Morty is an Adult Swim animation series and mentioning this is important because of the medium Rick and Morty represents itself in (animation). In this special episode, Rick and Morty utilises’ videogame modes such as pixel graphics, parallax two-dimensional scrolling, boss fights, and Morty as an upgradable weapon system throughout their adventure. All of which fit the description of intermediality, although does it do the same for gamification?

Well according to (Deterding et al 2016) contrasting and comparing games and play with the help of Caillois's concept of paidia and ludus is extremely important to understanding gamification. Because paidia (‘playing’) refers to a free form and expressive improvisational combination of behaviours and Ludus meaning (‘gaming’) structured by rules and strife towards goals. Therefore with this definition in mind and considering our Rick and Morty case study I can conclude that what distinguishes transmediality and gamification is that although Rick and Morty uses elements that are characteristic to games and play a significant role in gameplay, the very fact that Rick and Morty are represented as an animation series mostly resembles play, because the animators are the people who had free expression over the medium and not the viewer (Deterding et al 2016) states videogames unlike any other medium actively provides a point of interaction for users to infer their meaning towards. As such intermediality should not be construed with gamification because the subject would have to fit the definition of a game as a result.

Therefore, to say something is gamified, we would have to begin by defining a game and consider how they are designed. Thus, to determine if a subject has been gamified one would have to distinguish the interface design patterns, mechanics, conceptual modes, and general design principles used to structure games and consider whether the subject in question is created and developed with the intention of a system that includes the Ludus elements of gaming.
