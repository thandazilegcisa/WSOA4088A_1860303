Research Essay

In the history of games studies, game mechanics are thought of as a key factor in establishing the relation between the rules and the challenges of the system. Understanding this distinction is important because according to Sicart (2008) research on games as systems leads to a formal analysis of the game components and how they interrelate. This paper will attempt to define games and address what constitutes a game, why, and how gameful experiences can be materialised or engaged with in relation to the mechanics or rules defined by a system. This will serve to define and situate what gamification means in relation to my research context – Surveys. Therefore, the objective of this paper and my practical component is to propose gamification as a service to make surveys more pleasant to fill and consequently improve the quality of survey performance by evoking the positive or negative experiences and motivations that games do.

The first section of this paper sets out to describe the methodologies practiced while conducting research. The second section defines and discusses games and then uses the definitions to reflect on how something be it a classroom, workplace environment, product, or in our case study Surveys can be gamified in section three. Section four reflects on these definitions and relates them to my project. And lastly section five concludes the document.

1. Methodology The methodology adopted for this research project is largely based on the collection of secondary data, which has guided both my understanding of my research as practice and theory. Hence, the foundation of both the practical, theoretical component stems from seeking to define and establish the meaning of Games, and Gamification. For example, I have used the MDA framework throughout the process of making my prototype and defining games in the following section along with game studies literature that I will use.

2. Defining Games: The MDA framework is a widely used game design framework that assists designers/developers in understanding the relationship between the system and the agency that it provides to describe gameful experiences that encapsulate desirable emotional responses evoked by the player when they interact with a game system. However, throughout history, the main challenge faced by many researchers in establishing the significance of rules in relation to mechanics.

Previous definitions include understanding mechanics as a rule-based system that facilitate and encourage a user to explore and learn the properties of their possibility space using feedback mechanisms” (Sicart 2008). And as “a particular set of rules available to the player in the form of prescribed causal relations between game elements and their consequence to particular game states” (Sicart 2008).

The interesting thing about these definitions is that they introduce the idea that a game is nothing without a player and that the player must be realised and sometimes embodied through the design of the system. Therefore, important to the discussion of games is constraints and the constraints, be it semantically through the environment or the different game states that a game exhibits. Therefore, game mechanics can be understood as methods invoked by agents (the player), to subsequently interact with the rules to change the conditions of a game state (Sicart 2008).

Hence, “game mechanics are concerned with the actual interaction with the game state, while rules provide the possibility space where that interaction is possible and thereby regulate the transition between states e.g., having Hp in a game is a mechanic, however, the rule would be how many lives or health points a player has in a specific game state. Rules, therefore, affect the runtime behaviour of how the mechanics are contextualised. In other words, mechanics to my understanding are affordances provided by a system, whereas rules are constraints that constitute the various challenges that can be solved using the mechanics of a system.

Sicart (2008) also presents a definition that signifies the systems that make up a game. He contextualises this argument using an object-orientated framework from which mechanics are programmed in a system to signify behaviour. I find this very interesting because this has guided much of how I went about programming the technical side of the project, I mainly used what he describes as object orientated programming. According to Sicart (2008) “following object-orientated programming terminology, a method is understood as the actions or behaviours available to a class. And methods are essentially the mechanisms an object has for accessing data within another object”.

Thus, the jump method is a mechanic because as defined by Sicart (2008) “game mechanics are methods invoked by agents for interacting with the game world”. This reflects the point of view undertaken in pursuit to gamify a survey. 3. Defining Gamification: Gamification has grown into becoming a largely contested term since its first introduction in 2008. However, there is evidence that gamification plays a crucial role in designing for motivation in a non-gaming context, products, and services (Deterding 2012). Therefore, in its earlier conception gamification has become synonymous with rewards. Hence, most early adopters of gamification systems tended to focus on adding points, levels, leader boards, achievements, or badges to a real-world setting to entice people to engage with a product or service.

However, researchers argue that reward-based gamification “takes the least essential element in games and represents it as the core of the experience” (Deterding 2012). This contends that the pleasures of games arise not from a feedback system such as rewards, but meaningful choices (Deterding 2012). This is significant because Nicholson (2011) reiterates this idea by contextualising the use of gamification in relation to the type of goals set out to be achieved. He distinguishes the contextual needs for inspiring short-term, and long-term goals. Thus, in establishing this difference I would argue that feedback systems often aim to signify the achievement of short-term goals of a player.

Therefore, long-term goals often aim to intrinsically motivate players to make meaningful decisions by providing gameful experiences. What are gameful experiences? Well since games today often take upwards of 15 hours to complete. Some long-term transformations or processes take place as the player learns how to control the system and actively define their experience within the game. As a result, mechanics in the long term do not become a heavy cognitive load, instead, the game experience comes to the fore. Therefore, from this, I would argue that a gameful experience mostly speaks to the dynamic, aesthetic qualities that games employ, such as enjoyment, challenge, sensation, narrative, affect, unintended consequences, and flow – to name a few.

Deterding et al (2008) define gamification as the use of game design elements in non-game contexts. We have already discussed some of those elements, some of which take the form of reward-based, feedback systems. Although, my focus for the project was to establish a mechanical foundation from which I can understand and subsequently implement gamification. And in that respect, although jumping and running are not specific to games, it is characteristic to games in the context of a jump and run infinite scroller and consequently, these are elements that are found in most games, including more specifically infinite runners or platformers. Therefore, the basis from which I have approached to gamify surveys is from a systems perspective, more specifically mechanics.

Therefore, my practical component emphasises a systematic understanding of gamification, instead of the experiential nature of games. Hence, the component does not aim to understand the value creation in services. Therefore, according to Houtari and Hamari’s (2010) definition, my practical component does not build business intelligence. However, my practical component can be viewed as part of the overall service of surveys and how it supports the core service offering, which is to provide a challenging or gameful experience for the player.

4. Gamification of Online Surveys In my own experience, though surveys are an important means of data collection in research and marketing, they are often dull and unengaging. Therefore, for this project I have proposed gamification as means to make online surveys more pleasant to fill and consequently, to improve the quality of surveys using providing motivational affordances using game mechanics. According to Harms et al (2015), this effective behaviour towards surveys drives “negative respondent behaviour such as speeding, random responding, premature, and lack of attention”. However, upon inspection “gamified surveys have reported diverse benefits regarding user experience, motivation, participation, amount and quality of data.

As mentioned before, my practical component predominantly focuses on the systems perspective of gamification rather than service or business intelligence, therefore there are no clear target user groups determined for the process. Although, the practical component focuses on mechanics, because of the nature of infinite runner games, inciting challenges as motivational means of engaging with the system was the intended goal for the aesthetics. For the sake of this paper, I will detail and reflect on the decisions I took in deciding how the challenge has or can be further implemented and how its implementation will affect the quality or randomness of responses.

The MDA framework defines dynamics as “the run-time behaviour of a system”. So, dynamics imply change, how a user’s actions affect the overall state of gameplay. Therefore, to communicate the completion of a flow of interactions I have created a feedback loop in the vein of a progress bar. This is the only feedback mechanism I have incorporated into the project, mainly because a progress bar fits the context that I am working in – Surveys.
