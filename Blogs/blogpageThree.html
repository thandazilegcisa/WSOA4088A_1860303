Why Gamification is Bullshit?

In the paper of the above title, Ian Bogost examines and explores why gamification is bullshit, thus this paper seeks to reflect on the said reading by evaluating the propositions posed by Ian Bogost. Bogost makes it clear that what he means by “bullshit” is not derogatory. Instead, through it he merely suggests that “gamification is a primary practice of marketers and consultants who seek to construct and exploit an opportunity for benefit”(Bogost 2011).

Exploitation is an important term to note to help understand how he rations “bullshit”. Because he writes that, “bullshit is a coercive strategy rather than one that has anything to do with the true value or promise of something”. He continues by providing context to his argument, by situating the ‘gamifier’ as a consultant. And identifies the consultant as a “bullshitter” that promotes the uses of gamification as an attempt to appeal to business as how executives can bolster or improve a company’s business model. The premise of Bogost’s argument is that gamification designers “bullshit” business executives into believing that gamification is a solution for achieving their goals.

It is for this reason that Bogost suggests that gamification is the goal of gamification. And what I understand from this point is that he identifies and positions gamification consultants as designers of a persuasive game. In his 2007 book titled “Persuasive Games: The Expressive Power of Videogames”. Ian Bogost defines persuasive games as “those that aim to change or improve the attitudes of its players”. From this definition, we can deduce that he cites the persuasive acumen of consultants as a gamified business model sold to businesses to satisfy a company’s desire to claim that it has a gamification strategy.

As such during the past decade theories on and about gamification have become increasingly popular and have ultimately expanded into areas beyond the usual scope of earlier uses of points, leaderboards, and badges. Therefore, considering the early uses of gamification, Bogost notes that consultants “bullshit’ businesses by using similar or often the same gamification strategy across the concerned companies. And suggests that the use of points, leader boards and badges contribute to the “bullshitting” of businesses. As a result such gamification strategies do not consider the business model of business, because they do not provide any rationale behind the solution instead such strategies “assume that the fundamentals of a business including their business model, is sound”. And employees just need to be coerced into performing. For this reason, I agree with Bogost’s statement because such gamification strategies deliver short-term results. Results indicate and improve on the empirical performance of the business entity and not the individual.

However, during the latter period of the past, last decade theories such as meaningful gamification have come into the conversation. Talking points include how gamification can be designed to bolster meaningful intrinsic motivation. Thus, intrinsic motivation prioritises the individual and their experiences. Therefore, bolstering intrinsic motivation would mean that the success of gamified solutions cannot be quantified by the empirical evidence of the businesses’ performance. Instead, they should be quantified by the qualitative evidence of the space, or ‘virtual space’ provided by gamification. And how the employees' experience and function within the space. To end up as intrinsically motivated employees.

Therefore, I am not completely of the opinion that gamification is “bullshit” but agree that gamification is bullshit because as explored in my previous literature review consultants may simply be trying to replicate the ‘aura’ of games for additional exhibition value. Therefore, “bullshit” is the result of the fact that designers were previously persuading businesses to adopt gamification practices for the wrong reasons, as outlined above.
